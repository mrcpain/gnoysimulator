The World

An alt-Earth present day. Familiar enough to hurt.

Officially, everyone here is equal. There's a flag for it, ads about it, and a national holiday where every brand recolors its logo to say so. Unofficially, and you know this the way you know which stair creaks, there are exactly two kinds of people: the ones who own things, and you.

The owners have a word for your kind. They don't say it to your face; they say it in the meeting, in the margin, in the metadata. You're a Gnoy, one head of the great contented herd, the Gnoym, the demographic the entire apparatus exists to feed. It isn't a slur. It's worse than a slur. It's a category. It's printed on the forms. You qualify for the Gnoy rate, the Gnoy plan, the Gnoy-friendly redesign. Nobody ever sat you down and explained that you were one. Nobody had to. That's the elegant part.

What they feed the herd is slop, and the word is doing honest work. Slop is anything engineered to go down easy, never fill you, and keep you chewing: the meal, the feed, the subscription, the outrage timed to land right before bed. The faction that manufactures it is named, with no embarrassment whatsoever, The Trough. They are not hiding the metaphor. They've run the numbers on hiding it and found it isn't necessary. You keep eating.

The ones holding the ladle are the Xyoners, five machines (food, media, faith, enforcement, money) that carve up the world the way wolves carve up a field. They compete viciously for your attention and never once for your obedience; on the matter of the herd, they are in perfect agreement. And all five answer upward to the same small, much older table, which the awakened will tell you is either a board of directors or something a great deal older and worse. Gnoy Simulator is about the morning the slop tastes like what it is, the herd starts to look like a herd, and you commit the one act a Gnoy is never supposed to be capable of: you notice.

★ Twelve districts you can walk

The city has twelve neighborhoods and not one of them is really for you. There's the district where you sleep, the district where you work, the district where you're sold things, and the district where the people who sold them to you keep the money afterward. You can walk every inch of it. You will not be welcome in most of it.

Each block keeps its own Heat: a quiet tally of how much trouble you've been lately. Make noise in one and the others stay calm, which is the closest thing to mercy the map offers. And the city only ever shows you what your eyes are open enough to see. The back-alley shortcuts and the camera blind spots were always there. You just weren't awake enough to find them yet.

The Five Factions of the Xyoners

Five different machines. One arrangement.

Five machines own everything a Gnoy will ever touch: what you eat, what you watch, what you're told to believe, who gets to arrest you, and what the whole arrangement costs you by the month. They compete ferociously for your attention and not once for your obedience, on the herd, they agree. They call the understanding The Arrangement, and they would be genuinely hurt to hear you call it a conspiracy. It isn't one. It's just good business, and you are the business. Each is a wing of the story you can investigate, infiltrate, or burn down, and almost everything later on this page is something one of them is doing to you right now.

⌬

Food & Land

The ones who feed you and own the dirt your apartment sits on. Fast-food empires, agribusiness combines, the developers who priced out the neighborhood three mayors ago.

★ Above the Five ★

THE INNER RING: "The Arrangement"

The five factions are not the top of anything. Above them sits the Inner Ring, a membership of unknown size whose names are redacted out of every document you will ever pull. The Trough, the Feed, the Pulpit, the Cage, and the Vault are only its hands. What is on the record is bad enough: they take land, they buy elections, they keep heads of state on a leash by file, and the youngest names in the city's missing-persons rolls have an ugly habit of belonging to the wrong neighborhoods. The Ring does not deny any of it. It simply never appears in the photograph.

What the Ring actually is never makes it onto any record. The deepest forum threads, and a few of your own Inner Voices once your Awakening runs high enough, swear it is older than the country and older than money: an appetite the oldest scripture warns about and no congregation takes literally, a thing the believers flatter with the name the Sated One so that it might, someday, finally be. Fed children quietly at the center, the theory goes, and everyone else slowly, on subscription. You cannot tell whether you are fighting a three-thousand-year-old cult or very rich people who found the costume useful. After long enough, that stops mattering. The game never confirms a word of it.

What is certain: one of them has been feeding you intelligence since the first case, grooming you to gut their rivals for them. You will not learn which one until you decide it is over.

The Premise

You start as a model Gnoy. You will not finish that way.

Your alarm sells you something. You believe it. You eat a fridge-cold breakfast from a brand you didn't choose. You scroll a feed you didn't ask for on the commute. Seven subscriptions auto-deduct from your account every month. You clock into a desk job at Greystone Data Solutions. By every available metric, you are doing fine.

Then a news event happens in front of you. You watch it happen. You watch the same event on the Feed that night. They are not the same event. The footage has been edited. A coworker asks what you think. Whatever you answer permanently locks his fate. And the game, very quietly, begins.

SCHRÖDINGER'S NPC

The first thing you say to Dave determines who he is for the whole game.

A coworker. He asks what you think about the edited news segment. Your answer, before you understand the stakes, permanently decides his path: a forever-compliant Gnoy who stays out of your way, your first recruit and the most loyal member of your cell, or a Glowie the Cage planted from day one specifically to watch you. You will not know which one you chose until it is far too late. He is only one character. The game has a lot of Daves.

Gameplay Loop · A Day

Every day, you spend four slots. The game costs you the rest.

Time is the scarcest resource in the game. Each day breaks into four time slots. You pick what to do in each one. Sleep advances the clock, ticks the world forward, deducts your subscriptions, restores your Cope. Whatever you don't choose, you don't get back.

SIGNATURE MOMENT

The Subscription Cancellation Ritual

Here, canceling a subscription is a tiny act of disobedience, and the system treats it like one. You open your bills, tap "Cancel SloppFlix," and grind through three retention screens, a guilt-trip popup, and a phone tree designed to make you give up. You confirm anyway. A typewriter stamp slams the page. Within the hour, The Feed runs a segment on the "growing trend of anti-subscription extremism," and somewhere in a government file a flag with your name on it goes up. You woke up a little; the world noticed. That trade, small private defiance, oversized public consequence, is the whole game in miniature.

LOCKED TENSION

Slop heals you instantly. It also kills you slowly.

Fast food restores BODY mid-combat with no in-moment penalty. The cost is Slop Damage, a separate accumulation track that drifts your attributes down over the campaign, visibly changing your character's posture and skin. You can detox with Anti-Slop and Gymmaxx investment, but never fully reverse it. The system rewards short-term compliance with long-term decay. That is not a metaphor. It is the damage formula.

Gameplay Loop · Conversation

Talking is a system. Every word is a save.

Conversations are first-class gameplay. You see how hard each line is to pull off up front, a difficulty number the game calls the DC, but never how it will land. Up to 24 named Inner Voices can interrupt you mid-conversation, each with its own typography and personality. You can drop physical items from your pocket into a conversation to unlock lines you couldn't otherwise reach. Two villains are fought entirely through dialogue, multi-stage composure duels where the wrong framing costs you evidence and Heat.

Gameplay Loop · Combat

Real-time. Top-down. Extremely lethal.

Combat is fast and brutal. Restart in under half a second when you die, but the world remembers (broken doors stay broken, the bodies don't move). Stats modify the dials but never grant invincibility. Body is your hit threshold. Gymmaxx is your move speed. Hands is your damage. Ghost Mode unlocks a stealth first-strike. When you go down, you choose: YIELD for a managed capture (you wake up in a Cage holding cell, with one playable escape attempt) or ATTEMPT ESCAPE for a single fast risky roll.

The enemy adapts. The faction Dossier reads your build and dispatches counter-tactics on the next encounter, stealth-heavy gets thermal sensors, broadcast-heavy gets pre-emptive misinfo, melee-heavy gets ranged-only squads. There is no correct build. There are only builds the world has prepared a response for.

★ Four ways to fight dirty

Melee
Loud · Cheap · Honest

Pipe wrench, bat, crowbar, fire extinguisher, a skateboard, a heavy enough book. Whatever's bolted to the wall or lying in the alley. Scales with Hands, the working man's end of an argument.

Non-Lethal
No Heat · No Body

Taser, tranq dart, pepper spray, sleep gas, net launcher. Puts a target down without putting them in the ground, and without spiking your Heat. The pacifist's kit, and also the kidnapper's.

Ranged · Lethal
Max Heat · No Undo

Pistols, a shotgun, an SMG you can only get by taking it off a Cage operative. The fastest way to redline a whole district's Heat. For the runs you've already decided not to walk away from.

Thrown & Traps
Limited · Situational

Molotovs, bricks, throwing knives, a well-timed distraction. A few per fight, gone when used. For making a doorway, a diversion, or a sudden change of everyone's plans.

★ All 24 skills, and what they're actually for

Body · what your knees can still do
  • Gymmaxx: Out-running the consequences, literally. Move speed, dodges, climbs, escape rolls; also slowly undoes what a decade of slop did to your body.
  • Hands: For when the conversation is over. Damage, disarms, finishers, and the quiet dialogue options that only open when everyone can see you'd win.
  • Anti-Slop: Detox as a stat. Reverses the Slop Damage rotting your Body and Mind, the unglamorous grind of eating something that didn't come in a branded bag.
  • IRL Build: Making things the store won't sell you. Crafts gear, assembles your signature tools, and keeps the homebase from being held together with tape.
Mind · pattern recognition as a vice
  • Rabbit Hole: How far down a thread you can go before it follows you home. Governs all-night research benders; the deeper you get, the more the wall starts to make sense.
  • [X] Fatigue: Weaponized burnout. The more exhausted and cynical you are, the faster you clock the Xyoner tell in a press release. Being tired is, technically, a build.
  • Doxcraft: Turning a vacation photo into a home address. Leaked records, deanonymization, and the EXIF data nobody remembered to scrub.
  • Edit Farm: Fighting slop with its own tools. Fake a normie post, doctor a screenshot past the Feed's filters, build a cover story that survives a glance.
Soul · the part they can't buy yet
  • Glowie Sense: The prickle that says the loudest guy in the group chat is on someone's payroll. Sniffs out shills, feds, and controlled opposition. Sometimes correctly.
  • Yap Game: Being right out loud. Decides whether a speech, broadcast, or publication actually lands instead of being technically true to an empty room.
  • Lore Depth: You've read the footnotes nobody reads. Decodes Xyoner theology, banker dog-whistles, and the symbolism on the back of the slop packaging.
  • Based Talk: Waking another person up one honest sentence at a time. The only skill that turns a Gnoy into a recruit instead of a witness.
Mouth · social power, weaponized
  • Rizz: Raw magnetism, deployed responsibly or otherwise. Makes people like you, trust you, and hand you keys your other stats can't cash a check for.
  • NPC Mode: Performing a convincing Gnoy on command. Nod, smile, agree the coverage was balanced. The higher it goes, the longer you last in a room that wants you gone.
  • Ratio: Publicly dismantling someone in front of an audience. Demolishes a rival's credibility, a bad publication, or a boss's composure, out loud, on the record.
  • Clout: How far your words travel and how hard they hit on landing. Caps your Reach and decides whether the truth trends or dies in a feed nobody refreshes.
Ghost · being somewhere you aren't
  • Ghost Mode: Physical and digital invisibility. Silent movement, dropped tails, and sneak routes that only exist if you're quiet enough to find them.
  • Normie Cosplay: Looking and acting like you belong somewhere built to keep you out. The lanyard, the posture, the dead-eyed lunch-line shuffle.
  • Receipts: Proof that survives a denial. Camera discipline, secure storage, chain of custody, the difference between "I saw it" and "here it is."
  • OPSEC: Buying time against the inevitable. Doesn't make you invisible to the Cage, just delays the morning your name reaches the top of their file.
Signal · taking back the megaphone
  • Web: Who you know in the dark. The size and quality of your underground contacts; gates introductions to specialists you can't recruit alone.
  • Ghost Protocol: Keeping your comms off the Xyoners' map. Encrypts your backups so a capture costs you a night in a cell, not your whole case.
  • Sneaky Links: Moving things hand to hand so nothing's ever in writing. Dead drops, couriers, and off-grid money the Vault can't watch.
  • Signal Hijack: Taking the megaphone from the people who own all the megaphones. Commandeers Xyoner towers, the skill that makes pirate radio possible.

That's six attributes of four. The seventh, Gut, has no skills at all. It just quietly governs your Cope, your composure under fire, and whether you get back up after going down. The body keeps the score.

AWAKE GNOY PASSIVE REBEL

Two disposition axes emerge from your choices, independent of any single stat. GNOY ↔ AWAKE measures how clearly you see the machinery. PASSIVE ↔ REBEL measures whether you act on it.

Your position on the quadrant gates which endings are even available to you. Most builds drift naturally, but a high-Awakened Passive player and a high-Awakened Rebel player are playing two entirely different endgames.

Gameplay Loop · Build a Cell

Recruit specialists. Hope nobody breaks.

Progression · The Awakening

It is not a level-up screen. It is a slow tearing.

There is no XP bar for this. The Awakening is a hidden track from one to ten, and the game will never once show you the number, it makes you feel it instead. The color drains out of branded spaces a little more each level. The cheerful mall music starts hitting wrong notes, then skipping, then rotting toward silence. Your HUD develops a stutter. Resistance scrawl bleeds into the edges of menus that used to be clean. The interface itself is waking up at exactly your pace, and neither of you can be re-sold the dream once you've seen the wires behind it.

★ The Fatigue Tradeoff: the longer you push without rest, the higher your Fatigue climbs. At peak Fatigue you are a better investigator (pattern recognition sharpens, hidden connections surface) and a worse social operator (the "talks crazy" tag spreads through the Reputation Web, and NPCs who trusted you start to doubt). Being right and being believable are separate resources. Burnout is mechanically useful and socially destructive at the same time.

The World Watches Back

Three different systems remember you. None of them forget.

▣
System 01

The Dossier

One file. You never get to read it.

That's the whole point. The Cage keeps a single shared file on you, fed by operative reports, your own publications, and any Gnoy who saw your face and later got interrogated. You only feel it second-hand: more checkpoints as your Heat climbs, squads that arrive pre-built to counter your exact playstyle, Feed segments that weaponize your own evidence patterns. Lean on one tactic too long and the file learns you. OPSEC buys delay; it never buys invisibility.

⟁
System 02

The Politburo

It runs whether you log in or not.

Thirteen bosses, five factions, and the Inner Ring, running as a real simulation that ticks forward every week with or without you. They get promoted, betray each other, call in blackmail, and fill the empty chair when one goes down. Your moves are inputs, not scripts: take out the Innovation Czar and the sim decides who climbs into his seat, who tries to stop them, and whether the Cage exploits the mess. Play kingmaker from the shadows, leak to a rival, plant a false file, set the Cage and the Feed on each other, then watch it resolve on its own terms.

⌖
System 03

The Reputation Web

People remember your exact words.

Every Gnoy you talk to keeps a private memory of exactly what you said, and they gossip, a rumor you start in The Hollow takes real in-game weeks to surface across town. It's all diegetic: sit at your apartment laptop, re-read your own conversation log, catch where the Feed misquoted you, or notice that a throwaway line from three weeks ago is about to get someone killed. The friendliest bartender in the game is also a witness.

Here's why three is worse than one: you publish a story → a witness who once heard you talk gets interrogated → their memory of you drops into your Dossier word-for-word → the Politburo reads that updated file and builds next week's response around it. Three separate systems, one tightening noose, and not one of them needed a script to come looking for you.
The Cast of Villains

Thirteen operatives. Each one only dies once.

Some you beat with documents. Some with dialogue, in a multi-stage composure duel where the wrong framing costs you evidence and Heat. Some by handing them to a rival faction and letting them eat each other. Defeat is permanent. The Politburo fills the role with a successor, but that individual is gone forever.

How It Ends

Five endings. You decide when. The game does not push you.

Nobody fires a starting gun. There's no point-of-no-return cinematic, no quest marker that reads FINALE. When your read on the Inner Ring is sharp enough and your disposition sits where it needs to, you decide it's over and you move. The game will not tell you you're ready. It will not tell you you're wrong.

I
The Public Reckoning

You take it all public at once and the dam breaks. For forty-eight hours the Feed can't find the narrative and the whole country gets to see the wires. What anyone does with that is, terrifyingly, no longer up to you.

II
The Shadow Replacement

You don't burn the Arrangement down. You take its chair. The machine runs exactly as before, fed, obedient, only now your hand is on the lever, and you tell yourself that's different.

III
The Burn

You destroy everything you can reach and accept you won't be remembered correctly, if at all. Some fires aren't lit to win. They're lit so the next person can see by them.

IV
The Long Game

You walk away from the war and build something small, real, and slow. You disappear into it. The most patient ending, and depending on the day, either the wisest or the most cowardly.

V
The Sleep

You put it down. You re-subscribe; the first month is free for returning friends. The colors come back. The music resolves. The voices go quiet, one by one, until the apartment is clean and silent. The credits roll on a man who is, by every available metric, doing fine. The game does not punish you for this. It does not editorialize. It was always a real ending, and it was always right there.

★ Art Direction

Two visual languages. One satire.

Branded spaces are oversaturated, too vibrant, too clean. Resistance spaces are aged paper, hand-typed, marker-and-pencil annotated, slightly crooked. Both share one component grammar underneath. Both decay as you Awaken. The HUD does not narrate, the world does.

★ Xyoner UI

Corporate.
Cheerful.
Wrong.

Geometric sans. Polished cards. Drop shadows. The aesthetic of an app you didn't choose to install. Everything is fine. Everything is in compliance. Everything is on sale.

★ Resistance UI

Handmade.
Typewritten.
True.

Aged paper. Red marker. Sticky notes. Index cards. Pirate-broadcast green. The aesthetic of a private investigator's bathroom. Slightly crooked, because the truth never sits straight.
