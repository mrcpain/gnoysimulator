Gnoy Simulator Production Build Overview — A Plain-English Tour of How the Game Gets Made

Welcome, friend. This is the official corporate-approved™ summary of the fourteen big chunks of work (plus one art-production track) that, when stitched together, become the game on your screen. Read it with a Big Slop in hand. Have a great day.

Receipts Below this line, the Resistance gets the floor. What follows is the real document.
What this is. Building a video game is a lot like building a house — you can't paint the walls before you pour the foundation, and you can't hang art before there are walls. Gnoy Simulator is being assembled in 15 chunks we call epics. Each epic delivers one major thing the player can actually feel — a working day cycle, a fight, a conversation, an investigation, a district to walk through.

Some epics are foundations (you'll never see them directly, but nothing works without them). Others are moments (the things that show up in a trailer). The list below is roughly the order they get built in, because each one depends on the ones above it.

Epic 1 · Foundations & Systems

Foundation, Scaffolding & RPG Core

"Turn the lights on and put the bones in the body."

Before anything else, the game needs a skeleton. This epic creates the project itself: the empty Godot project, the save/load system, and the underlying RPG numbers — the 7 attributes (Body, Mind, Soul, Mouth, Ghost, Gut, Signal), 24 skills, 40 talents, and the Awakening Track that measures how "red-pilled" the player has become. It also wires in the skill-check engine (the thing that rolls dice when the player tries to do something), keyboard controls, and the minimal HUD frame you see on screen at all times.

Why it's first: Every other epic — combat, dialogue, investigation — reads from these numbers. Build this wrong and the whole game wobbles.

Epic 2 · Gameplay Loops

The Day Cycle & Survival Loop

"Wake up. Slop. Work. Sleep. Repeat — until you stop."

This is where the game becomes a life. Each day is broken into Morning / Afternoon / Evening / Night slots, and the player has to spend those slots — investigating, working their soul-crushing job at Greystone, recovering, or going out at night to take risks. Hit snooze and the Morning is silently gone. Rent and slop subscriptions auto-deduct from the bank account every month. Cancelling a subscription becomes a small ceremony (with three retention-screen guilt trips) and is rewarded with an Awakening tick. The "slop economy bleeding you dry" feeling lives in this epic.

Headline moment: Cancelling SloppFlix for the first time, getting an "anti-subscription extremism" Feed segment about you within the hour.

Epic 3 · Gameplay Loops

Combat (Hotline Miami DNA)

"Top-down, lethal, instant restart. Stats don't make you invincible."

The fighting layer. Combat is fast, brutally lethal, and restarts in under half a second when you die — but the world remembers (broken doors stay broken, corpses stay where they fell). Your stats tweak the dials (Body = how many hits you survive, Hands = how much damage you deal) but never grant invincibility. When you go down at zero Body, you choose: YIELD (guaranteed capture, manageable consequences) or ATTEMPT ESCAPE (a fast risky check). The enemy faction reads your fighting style and adapts — go stealth-only and they bring thermal sensors next time.

Signature satire: Six "weaponized objects" that change the game — an Evidence Brick stuns a normie for 4 seconds, a Slop Bag distracts them for 6, a Megaphone can actually make low-level enforcers defect.

Epic 4 · Gameplay Loops

Equipment & Crafting

"What you wear is who the world sees."

Fifteen equipment slots (head, face, hands, accessories, weapons, etc.) across five quality tiers — from gnoy-grade fast fashion to gear seized from Xyoner operatives. The interesting part is the set bonuses: dressing fully civilian gives you a "blends in" buff and faster Heat decay. Dressing fully tactical makes you a combat monster but kills your social viability — nobody talks to a guy in body armor. "Press Cover" gets you through Cage checkpoints. The outfit IS the disguise.

Crafting: Resistance gear isn't bought — it's built, in homebase, by you or your Crafter recruit, from scrap.

Epic 5 · Gameplay Loops

Investigation — The Three-Layer Conspiracy Wall

"Pin the photo. Draw the string. Stamp it. Publish it."

The heart of the game. Three nested screens: Physical Board — a literal corkboard in your apartment where you pin evidence and drag red string between connections (each string fires a skill check; succeed and the line is solid, fail and it's a dotted line you can still publish at a Credibility cost). Dossier Interface — your desk, where you stamp evidence Verified / Uncertain / Planted and build a publication: which platform, which framing angle, which audience. You see a live preview of how much Credibility you'll gain and how much Heat you'll bring down on yourself. Thought Cabinet — mental threads that cost time-slots to chew on and re-interpret evidence you already have.

The Truth Paradox: The more correct your investigation gets, the more dangerous and discredited you become. The game does not tell you when you're wrong; the Cage plants false leads on purpose.

Epic 6 · Gameplay Loops

Dialogue System (Disco-Elysium-Tier)

"Every word goes into a file somewhere."

Conversations are first-class gameplay. You'll see the difficulty of each line up front ("Yap Game DC 14 — Push back on his framing") but never the consequence. Inside your head, up to 24 named Inner Voices — Glowie Sense, Rabbit Hole, Lore Depth, Yap Game — chime in with their own fonts, colors, and (eventually) voice cues. As you Awaken and Fatigue rises, they get louder and more chaotic. You can drop physical evidence from your pocket onto the conversation (a photo, a burner phone) to unlock lines you couldn't otherwise say. Two bosses — the Trusted Anchor and the Debt Dealer — are fought entirely through dialogue, with a "Composure HP" bar.

Weaponized log: Every conversation is recorded and searchable. NPCs quote you back weeks later. The Cage interrogates the people you spoke to and your exact words flow into their dossier on you.

Epic 7 · World & Living Simulation

The Three Tracking Systems

"The world watches back."

The hardest engineering work in the project. Three legally and architecturally distinct systems that together make the world feel alive: (1) Player Dossier — a single faction-wide file the Cage assembles on you from operative reports, leaks, and interrogations. (2) Politburo Simulation — a weekly ticking simulation of the ruling class that runs whether or not you exist. Bosses succeed each other, betrayals happen, blackmail debts come due. (3) Reputation Web — each individual person remembers what you said to them and gossips with their neighbors. When the Cage interrogates one of them, their verbatim memory of you flows into your dossier.

Why it matters: This is what makes the world feel like it has its own life, and why two playthroughs can produce wildly different stories.

Epic 8 · World & Living Simulation

Recruitment & Homebase

"Build a cell. Hope nobody breaks."

The base-building / party arc. You start in a sad apartment. Through side quests and dialogue puzzles you recruit specialists — Researcher, Broadcaster, Crafter, Medic, Quartermaster, Analyst, Handler. Your home grows: Apartment → Office → Underground HQ → Distributed Cell Network. Loyalty is an ongoing resource that decays from neglect and broken promises. Recruits can be captured in raids, broken under interrogation, or quietly turned into Glowies (informants). Once recruits are gone, they're gone.

Beat: The Hollow district becomes your safehouse network. A high-loyalty Broadcaster unlocks pirate radio income.

Epic 9 · World & Living Simulation

World & Districts

"Twelve neighborhoods. Each one watches you differently."

The map. Twelve districts, each color-coded by faction influence — Slopside (Trough), the Cubicle Belt (Cage / mixed), the Plaza (Feed), the Vault District, the Garden (rich), the Pulpit Quarter, the Hollow (your safehouse), all the way up to The Compound (endgame). Heat is tracked per district: get loud in the Plaza and the Plaza heats up, but Slopside stays cool. Rotating between districts is core gameplay. Sneak routes only appear once Ghost Mode skill crosses a threshold. The map UI itself reveals more layers as you Awaken.

Vertical Slice: The first playable demo ships three districts — Slopside, the Cubicle Belt, and the Plaza — covering work, home, and broadcast.

Epic 10 · Story, Bosses & Opening

Quest System & the Endgame Trigger

"You decide when you have enough."

Seven kinds of quests run in parallel — the Main Quest ("The Arrangement"), 13 Faction Boss Investigations, Recruit Personal Quests, Emergent Politburo Events (the sim spits these out), Reputation Web side quests, Awakening Track story beats, and District Liberation Goals. There are no quest markers by default. You find leads by overhearing conversations, reading forum posts, getting tips from your Analyst, or stumbling onto dead drops. Your Quest Journal is written like a paper notebook in your character's own voice — not a checklist. When Inner Ring Confidence is high enough, you choose to trigger the endgame. The game does not push you there.

Accessibility: Markers Mode (optional, off by default) and Awakening Hints exist for players who want them.

Epic 11 · Story, Bosses & Opening

Boss Investigations

"Expose them. Permanently."

Each major Xyoner operative — the Fact Checker, the Trusted Anchor, the Debt Dealer, the Innovation Czar, the Pulpit's rotating-mask boss, the Glowie hiding inside your own recruits — is a multi-stage investigation arc with their own defeat condition. Some you beat with documents (Innovation Czar via leaked R&D papers); some with dialogue duels (Trusted Anchor); some via cross-faction manipulation (handing the Number Go Up Guy to the Cage). When a boss falls, the Politburo simulation fills the role with a successor — but that individual person is gone forever.

Solo-dev scope: 5–7 bosses ship at full launch (the Pulpit consolidates 4 sub-bosses into one rotating-mask figure). The remaining 6–8 stay on the design wall as "North Star" content if the project ever scales.

Epic 12 · Story, Bosses & Opening

The Opening Sequence & Schrödinger's Dave

"A Perfectly Normal Morning."

The first 15 minutes of the game, and the most carefully scripted sequence in the whole project. The player wakes up, snoozes (and silently loses the Morning slot — first lesson, no tutorial popup), eats slop from the fridge, scrolls the Feed, commutes past a billboard, clocks into Greystone Data Solutions, and has their first conversation with Dave. Dave is a "Schrödinger's NPC": the first answer you give him permanently locks his arc. Default gnoy answer = Dave stays a gnoy forever (the heartbreak of resistance). Real answer = Dave becomes your first Recruit. Too-awakened answer = Dave reports you to the Cage, and you discover Greystone was a Cage front all along.

Goal: By the end of opening, the player has been silently introduced to time-slots, slop damage, financial pressure, conversation consequences, the evidence board, and Feed-vs-reality — without a single tooltip.

Epic 13 · Polish, Endings & Accessibility

Theme System, Awakening Filter & Music Deterioration

"As you wake up, the world sounds wrong."

The aesthetic spine. Two visual languages share one component library: Xyoner (corporate, oversaturated, cheerful, geometric sans) and Resistance (handmade, typewritten, aged paper, marker-and-pencil). The duality itself is the satire. As your Awakening Track climbs, a post-process shader gradually desaturates the world — the bright corporate colors start reading as wrong. At the same time, the corporate music in Xyoner spaces audibly deteriorates across 5 tiers — a clean jingle becomes slightly off-key, then wobbly, then skipping, then full corrupted noise by Awakening level 7. The HUD itself glitches and decays. Resistance UI elements grow into your homebase.

Signature moment: The first time the McXyon's commercial in the background warbles for half a second and the player's head snaps up.

Epic 14 · Polish, Endings & Accessibility

Endings, Polish, Localization & Accessibility

"Five ways out (three at launch). Plus making it actually shippable."

The closing epic. Five possible endings exist as design (Public Reckoning, Shadow Replacement, The Burn, The Long Game, The Sleep). Solo-dev launch ships three: Public Reckoning (you go public, the dam breaks), The Long Game (you walk away to live a meaningful private life), and The Sleep (you give up, take your slop, and let the gnoy reabsorb you — a fully supported, non-punished ending). This epic also lands accessibility (WCAG AA — text scaling, dyslexia font, color-blind cues, subtitles, hold-to-confirm, full keyboard nav, screen-reader layer), localization-readiness (every string externalized, fonts that handle CJK / Arabic / Hebrew), final tone-calibration playtests, IP / legal review, and Steam Deck Verified certification at 60fps on minimum-spec hardware.

Sleep ending matters: The game is designed so that giving up is a valid ending. Marketing briefs reviewers on this — it's not a failure state.

Epic A · Art Production (Parallel Track)

Art Production — Sprites, Tilesets, UI, Portraits, Icons runs alongside everything

"Make the screen look like the design doc said it would."

The fifteenth track, and the only one that uses letters instead of numbers because it runs in parallel with all the others. Epic A doesn't write code — it produces the actual pictures: the player's wageworker sprite (idle and walk animations), Dave's three possible sprite variants, the Fact Checker boss sprite set, generic gnoy civilians, named NPCs, the apartment interior, the Greystone office, broadcast row, every HUD element, every UI surface, every portrait, every skill and item icon. Built through carefully locked image-generation pipelines (gpt-image-2, pixel-snap, atlas packing), shipped to canonical art folders for the code epics to consume.

Why parallel: Code epics can ship using "smoke-test" placeholder art and still validate. Epic A's job is to backfill the real, art-direction-locked assets in time for the Vertical Slice to look the way the design document promised.
